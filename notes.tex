\section{Fundamentals of Hamiltonian Dynamics}
\subsection{Notations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vect{q} = (q_1, \ldots, q_n)$: generalized coordinates
    \item $\dot{\vect{q}} = (\dot{q_1}, \ldots, \dot{q_n})$: generalized velocity
    \item $\Lcal(t, \vect{q}, \dot{\vect{q}}) = K - U = \frac12 \dot{\vect{q}}^\top M \dot{\vect{q}} - U(\vect{q})$: Lagrangian, where $M$ is the mass matrix, $K$ is kinetic energy and $U$ is potential energy
    \item $\vect{p}$: generalized momentum, i.e.,\ $\vect{p} = M \cdot \dot{\vect{q}}$
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Euler--Lagrange Equation}
Generalized version: given a funtion $\vect{y}(x)$ and its derivative: $\vect{y}'(x)$ and $f(x, \vect{y}, \vect{y}')$. Define the functional:
\begin{equation}
F[\vect{y}] = \int_a^b f(x, \vect{y}, \vect{y}') dx \label{eqn:functional}
\end{equation}
As the topic in calculus of variation, we want the functional $F[\vect{y}]$ to obtain its local min for some function $\vect{y}$. Perturbate $\vect{y}(x)$ with $\vect{y}(x) + \eps \vect{u}(x)$ for any function $\vect{u}$ and $\eps \in \R$ with small enough $\abs{\eps}$. and put \[g(\eps) = \int_a^b f(x, \vect{y + \eps u}, (\vect{y + \eps u})') dx\]
To make $F[\vect{y}]$ obtaining its local minimum for some $\vect{y}$ satisfying the initial conditions: $\vect{y}(a)$ and $\vect{y}(b)$ being fixed, i.e.,\ $\vect{u}(a) = \vect{u}(b) = 0$, it is equivalent to require $g'(0) = 0$, hence:
\begin{align*}
& g'(0) = \frac{d}{d\eps}\Big(\int_a^b f(x, \vect{y + \eps u}, (\vect{y + \eps u})') dx\Big)\\
& = \int_a^b \Big(\pd{f}{\vect{y}}\vect{u} + \pd{f}{\vect{y}'}\vect{u}' \Big)dx \quad \text{By total derivative}\\
& = \int_a^b \vect{u} \pd{f}{\vect{y}}  dx + \pd{f}{\vect{y}'}\vect{u} \vert_a^b - \int_a^b \vect{u} \frac{d}{dx}\big(\pd{f}{\vect{y}'}\big) dx \quad \text{integration by parts}\\
& = \int_a^b \vect{u} \Big(\pd{f}{\vect{y}} - \frac{d}{dx}\pd{f}{\vect{y}'}\Big) dx = 0 \quad \text{By initial condition:} ~ \vect{u}(a) = \vect{u}(b) = 0
\end{align*}
By the fundamental lemma of calculus of variation, since $\vect{u}(x)$ is arbitrary, hence we must have:
\begin{equation}
\pd{f}{\vect{y}} - \frac{d}{dx}\pd{f}{\vect{y}'} = 0 \label{eqn:Euler_Lagrangian}
\end{equation}
Equation (\ref{eqn:Euler_Lagrangian}) is called the \textbf{Euler--Lagrangian Equation} in calculus of variation, and for $\vect{y}$ satisfying equation (\ref{eqn:Euler_Lagrangian}) is a sufficient condition for $F[\vect{y}]$ to have its local minimum.

In a dynamical system, with Lagrangian $\Lcal(t, \vect{q}, \dot{\vect{q}})$, we replace $f$ with $\Lcal$, $\vect{y}$ with generalized coordiantes $\vect{q}$, and $x$ with time $t$, we get the \textbf{Lagrangian Equation} for dynamical system:
\begin{equation}
\pd{\Lcal}{\vect{q}} - \frac{d}{dt}\pd{\Lcal}{\dot{\vect{q}}} = 0 \label{eqn:Lagrangian} 
\end{equation}

\subsection{Legendre Transform}
If $f: \Xcal \to \R$ is convex, then $\forall t \in X$, then $f(t) \geq pt + b$, where $p = f'(x)$ and $b = f(x) - px$. This implies that:
\[pt - f(t) \leq px -f(x), \forall t \in \Xcal\]
Then the one-dimensional Legendre transform is defined as: 
\[f^*(p) = px - f(x) = \sup\{pt - f(t) : t \in \Xcal\}\]

If $\Xcal \subset \R^n$, and $\vect{p} = \nabla f = \pd{f}{\vect{x}}$, then the generalized Legendre tranform is defined as:
\[f^*(\vect{p}) = \vect{p} \cdot \vect{x} - f(\vect{x}) = \sup\{\vect{p}\cdot \vect{t} - f(\vect{t}) : \vect{t} \in \Xcal\}\]
where $\vect{p} \cdot \vect{x} = \vect{p}^{\top} \vect{x}$ representing the inner product. 

Note that $f^*$ is also a convex function, since given a $t \in \Xcal$, the map $p \mapsto pt -f(t)$ is linear, hence the supremum of the family of linear maps: $\{p \mapsto pt-f(t)\}_{t \in \Xcal}$ gives an envolope, which is $f^*$. On the other hand, one can show:
\[f(x) = \sup\{px - f^*(p) : p \in \Xcal^*\}\]
which means that $f(x)$ is the envolope of the family of linear maps: $\{x \mapsto px - f^*(p)\}_{p \in \Xcal^*}$. 



\subsection{Hamiltonian Equation}
Hamiltonian is the Legendre transform of Lagrangian, hence we have:
\begin{equation}
\Hcal(\vect{q}, \vect{p}) = \vect{p} \cdot \dot{\vect{q}} - \Lcal(t, \vect{q}, \dot{\vect{q}}) \label{eqn:Hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
Since $\vect{p}\cdot \dot{\vect{q}} = 2K$, so $\Hcal(\vect{q}, \vect{p}) = 2K - (K - U) = K + U$ and hence Hamiltonian can be interpreted as the total energy of a dynamical system. So (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian}) can also be written as:
\begin{equation}
\Hcal(\vect{q}, \vect{p}) = \frac12 \dot{\vect{q}}^\top M \dot{\vect{q}} + U(\vect{q}) = \frac12 \vect{p}^\top M^{-1} \vect{p} + U(\vect{q}) \label{eqn:Hamiltonian_2}
\end{equation}


We can derive Hamiltonian equations from Lagrange eqaution in (\ref{eqn:Lagrangian}). 

Note that by $\Lcal(t, \vect{q}, \dot{\vect{q}}) = \frac12 \dot{\vect{q}}^\top M \dot{\vect{q}} - U(\vect{q})$, we have:
\[\pd{\Lcal}{\dot{\vect{q}}} = M\cdot\dot{\vect{q}} = \vect{p} \quad \text{and by (\ref{eqn:Lagrangian})} \quad \pd{\Lcal}{\vect{q}} = \frac{d}{dt}\pd{\Lcal}{\dot{\vect{q}}} = \dot{\vect{p}}\]

so with (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian}) and (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_2}), we have:
\begin{align}
& \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{p}} = \dot{\vect{q}} \quad \text{and} \quad \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{p}} = M^{-1} \vect{p} \quad \label{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation1}\\
& \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{q}} = -\pd{\Lcal}{q} = - \dot{\vect{p}} \quad \text{and} \quad \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{q}} = \nabla_{\vect{q}} U(\vect{q})\quad \label{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation2}
\end{align}
(\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation1}) and (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation2}) are called \textbf{Hamiltonian equations}

Or equivalently, we can take the total differentiation on both sides of (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian}):
\begin{align*}
& d\Hcal = d(\vect{p}\cdot \dot{\vect{q}}) - d\Lcal(t, \vect{q}, \dot{\vect{q}})\\
& = \dot{\vect{q}}\cdot d\vect{p} + \vect{p} \cdot d\dot{\vect{q}} - \pd{\Lcal}{\vect{q}}d\vect{q} - \pd{\Lcal}{\dot{\vect{q}}}d\dot{\vect{q}} - \pd{\Lcal}{t}dt\\
& = \dot{\vect{q}}\cdot d\vect{p} - \pd{\Lcal}{\vect{q}}d\vect{q} - \pd{\Lcal}{t}dt\\
& = \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{p}}d\vect{p} + \pd{\Hcal}{\vect{q}} d\vect{q} + \pd{\Hcal}{t}dt
\end{align*}
Hence we can correspond the coefficients to get (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation1}) and (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation2}).

By combining (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation1}) and (\ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation2}), we obtain another form of Hamiltonian equations:
\begin{align}
& \dot{\vect{q}} = M^{-1} \vect{p} \label{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation3}\\
& \dot{\vect{p}} = - \nabla_{\vect{q}} U(\vect{q}) \label{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation4}
\end{align}
which will be uesd in the implementation of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo.

\section{Hamiltonian Monte Carlo}

\subsection{Leapfrog integrator}
The numerical approximation of Hamiltonian dynamics, which is a system of ODE: \ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation3}, \ref{eqn:Hamiltonian_equation4}, 
can be obtained by integrators. Particularly, the leapfrog integrator is the most preferred. 

\begin{align*}
p_{t+\eps/2} &= p_t - \frac{\eps}{2} \nabla U(x_t) \quad \text{(kick)}\\
x_{t+\eps} &= x_t + \eps M^{-1} p_{t+\eps/2} \quad \text{(drift)}\\
p_{t+\eps} &= p_{t+\eps/2} - \frac{\eps}{2} \nabla U(x_{t+\eps}) \quad \text{(kick)}\\
\end{align*}

If we expand the above, we could get the approximate state: $(\hat x_{t+\eps}, \hat p_{t+\eps})$ at time $t+ \eps$ from time $t$:
\begin{align*}
x_{t+\eps} &= x_t + \eps M^{-1} p_t - \frac{\eps^2}{2} M^{-1}\nabla U(x_t)\\
p_{t+\eps} &= p_t - \eps \nabla U(x_t) - \frac{\eps}{2}\Big(\nabla U(x_{t+\eps}) - \nabla U(x_t)\Big)\\
&= p_t - \eps \nabla U(x_t) - \frac{\eps^2}{2} \nabla^2 U(x_t) M^{-1} p_t + O(\eps^3)
\end{align*}
This is consistent with second-order Taylor expansion:
\begin{align*}
x_{t+\eps} &= x_t + \eps M^{-1} p_t - \frac{\eps^2}{2} M^{-1} \nabla U(x_t) + O(\eps^3)\\
p_{t+\eps} &= p_t - \eps \nabla U(x_t) - \frac{\eps^2}{2} \nabla^2 U(x_t) M^{-1} p_t + O(\eps^3)
\end{align*}

Since the energy is conserved in Hamiltonian dynamics, so it is natural to consider the Hamiltonian: $\Hcal(x, p) = C$ as a level set in phase space: $\R^d \times \R^d$, and the tangent space at state $(x_t, p_t)$ is naturally:
\[\text{span}\Big\{\Big(\frac{dx}{dt}, \frac{dp}{dt}\Big)^\top\Big\} = \text{span}\Big\{\Big(M^{-1} p_t, - \nabla U(x_t)\Big)^\top\Big\}\]
and the normal space is:
\[\text{span}\Big\{\Big(\pd{\Hcal}{x}, \pd{\Hcal}{p})^\top\Big\} = \text{span}\Big\{\Big(\nabla U(x_t), M^{-1} p_t\Big)^\top\Big\}\]

Consider starting from the current state $(x_t, p_t)$, we propose some tangent move: $\eps (M^{-1} p_t, - \nabla U(x_t))$, and the point being shot off the manifold:
\[\Big(\tilde x_{t+\eps}, \tilde p_{t+\eps}\Big) = \Big(x_t + \eps M^{-1} p_t, p_t - \eps \nabla U(x_t)\Big)\]
Note that the difference between $(\tilde x_{t+\eps}, \tilde p_{t+\eps})$ and the leapfrog solution of the state $(\hat x_{t+\eps}, \hat p_{t+\eps})$ at time $t + \eps$ is:
\[\Delta = -\frac{\eps^2}{2}\Big(M^{-1} \nabla U(x_t), \nabla^2 U(x_t) M^{-1} p_t\Big)\]

One question is: is this vector in the normal space of $(x_t, p_t)$. The answer is no in general. Because we need to enforce the condition:
\[
\Big(M^{-1} \nabla U(x_t), \nabla^2 U(x_t) M^{-1} p_t\Big)^\top = c \Big(\nabla U(x_t), M^{-1} p_t\Big)^\top 
\]
for some scalar $c$, which requires that $\nabla U(x_t)$ is the eigenvector of $M^{-1}$ and $M^{-1}p_t$ is the eigenvector of $\nabla^2 U(x_t)$ with the same eigenvalue $c$.

However, in some special cases, that can happen. Consider the simple harmonic osillator with $M = m$ and spring constant $k = \frac1m$, so $U(x) = \frac{k}{2} x^2$, then in this case, $\Delta \propto (kx/m, kp/m) $ and the normal vector is $ \propto (kx, p/m)$, therefore by setting $c = 1/m = k$ the requirements can be satisfied.
